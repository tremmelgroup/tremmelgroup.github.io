Cosmological Simulations of Galaxies and Supermassive Black Holes

Simulations are important tools for studying the evolution of galaxies and the supermassive black holes (SMBHs) Black holes that are typically millionts to billions of times the mass of our sun, found primarily in the centers of galaxies. We still don't know how they form or even exactly how many galaxies have a SMBH at their centers. at their centers. Our group uses cosmological hydrodynamic simulations Simulations that follow the formation and evolution of galaxies within a realistic, expanding Universe, including large scale structure and galaxy mergers. Hydrodynamic simulations include, in addition to dark matter and gravity, the physics of gas and star formation. to better understand the external (e.g. environment, mergers between galaxies) and internal (supernovae explosions, SMBHs) processes that shape the properties of galaxies throughout cosmic time.

Our group incorporates a variety of simulations and numerical techniques to conduct research. Our simulations utilize the most powerful supercomputers around the world, as well as new physics models that we develop. Our goal is to produce simulations that are capable of making new predictions relevant for the next generation of electromagnetic observatories (e.g. JWST), as well as current and future gravitational wave detectors (e.g. LISA, Pulsar Timing Arrays)

The Effects of Supermassive Black Holes on Galaxy Evolution

SMBHs can produce powerful outflows

When SMBHs grow they release tremendous amounts of energy, potentially affecting the evolution of their host galaxies. Using simulations, we study the connection between the growth and merger history of a galaxy and the activity of its SMBH. When and where do galaxies stop forming stars? Can energy released by SMBHs permanently suppress star formation?

Supermassive Black Holes in Merging Galaxies

When and where do SMBHs merge?

Future and on-going efforts to detect gravitational waves from merging, massive black holes will provide groundbreaking information about their origins and evolution. Understanding where and when such mergers take place, their local and large-scale environment, and any associated electromagnetic signatures will allow us to correctly interpret gravitational wave data and conduct follow-up observations.

Large-Scale Simulations

Follow the Evolution of Many Galaxies

Large-scale simulations, like Romulus (pictured on the left; courtesy of Prof. Tom Quinn), follow the evolution of thousands of galaxies in a variety of environments ranging from low mass dwarfs go massive galaxy groups and clusters. Romulus represents an important step in improving how we model SMBHs in such simulations, incorporating new models for SMBH growth, formation, and dynamics. An update to Romulus, called Amaroq, is currently being run by collaborators Professors Ben Keller and James Wadsley.

Zoom-in Simulations

A Focused, High Resolution Look at Galaxy Formation

Zoom-in simulations (image courtesy of Dr. Elaad Applebaum) focus on individual galaxies and can attain even higher resolution. We are developing the X-Gal Simulations, a new suite of zoom-in simulations that will incorporate the Genetic Modification technique to perform controlled experiments examining the effect that modifying the mass and merger histories of galaxies has on their properties and evolution.
