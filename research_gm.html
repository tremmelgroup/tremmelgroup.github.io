Genetically Modified Galaxies

Controlled experiments exploring the connection between dark matter halo assembly history and galaxy evolution

Controlled Experiments with Genetic Modification

Galaxies form in the centers of dark matter halos, large, gravitationally bound structures of dark matter. The evolution of dark matter halos and galaxies is strongly connected. Gas traces dark matter, so the faster a dark matter halo grows, so too is their more material to create a galaxy. More mergers between dark matter halos implies more mergers between galaxies as well. The Genetic Modification Technique, pioneered by the GM galaxies group led by Professor Andrew Pontzen at University College London, is able to manipulate specific aspects of a dark matter halo's assembly history, such as the number and mass ratio of its mergers, or its final mass, formation time, or rotation. It conducts these manipulations in a way that preserves each other aspect of the halo's properties. For example, we can change the mass ratio of a merger event without changing its final mass or its large scale environment. In collaboration with Andrew Pontzen and his group, we utilize this technique to conduct controlled experiments to test the influence a halo's assembly history on the evolution of the galaxy and supermassive black hole(s) that it hosts.

A New Suite of Zoom-in Simulations

It is common for simulators to run a number of high resolution, zoom-in cosmological simulations of individual galaxies. Our group does things slightly differently. Rather than run a suite of completely different galaxies, we instead use Genetic Modification to explore different variations on the same galaxy's assembly history. Combining this with testing different model variations, we have a powerful set of controlled experiments relating the merger and mass growth history of a galaxy to its evolution and that of its supermassive black hole(s).

An Important Laboratory for Testing New Physics Models

There is a tremendous amount of uncertainty around how supermassive black holes grow and interact with their environment. Implementing new models into our simulations in order to make new predictions for observations is a major priority of our group. Genetic Modification allows us to examine now only how different SMBH models affect the evolution of galaxies, but also how this may change the way galaxies are affected by their formation history. In other words, it is possible that two different models can create galaxies that looks similar in many ways. However, it may be that one model is affected more (or less) by variation's to its host galaxy's assembly history. Such experiments add a new dimension to testing new models for SMBH physics.
